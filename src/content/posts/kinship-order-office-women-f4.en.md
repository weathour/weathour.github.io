## Opening

When I read the post “Ancestral Halls and Surname Rights,” I think two mistakes are especially easy to make. The first is to shrink the issue into ancestral halls, genealogies, and which surname a child takes, as if the structure itself would dissolve once those visible symbols recede. The second is to moralize the whole question, as if women’s actual position would automatically improve once people become a little more enlightened.

Both judgments touch something real, but neither is enough. The more accurate object is not one ancestral hall, and not one surname convention. It is a broader order of kinship, property, and recognition. By that I mean the way a person is inserted into relational positions such as daughter, wife, daughter-in-law, mother, or married-out daughter; the way family property, intergenerational support, bride price, housing, care work, and return-home obligations are allocated through those positions; and the way that entire allocation is then packaged as natural, respectable, stable, and common sense.

As long as that order still organizes resources, time, and status, the problem has not disappeared simply because ancestral halls are less visible. It has changed scenes. For many educated women working in offices in provincial capitals, the ancestral hall looks distant, but surnames, marriage housing, domestic labor, childcare, holiday visits, elder care, parental support, and emotional coordination are all closer than ever.

The point of this essay is not to produce more anxiety. It is to answer three questions carefully. First, do these problems objectively exist in China today? Second, how do they appear differently in cities, county seats, and rural areas? Third, if the audience is educated women working office hours in provincial capitals, what kind of practical route is realistic rather than merely consoling?

This essay mainly relies on official statistics, legal and judicial materials, governance cases, and one public text sample from `2024-2026`.[1][2][3][4]

## 1. The object cannot be reduced to an “ancestral hall problem”

The strength of “Ancestral Halls and Surname Rights” is that it correctly senses that ancestral halls, genealogies, and surname conventions are not neutral cultural decorations. They are leftover forms of a patrilineal order.[14] But the limits of the post are equally clear. It assumes that once these forms recede on the surface, women no longer need to worry about winning recognition from men in the family, and it places major hope in technological progress, civilization, and changing ideas.[14]

The main problem with that move is not bad intention. It is a narrow object definition. What remains structurally stable is not one ancestral hall itself, but a composite order made of several linked parts:

- the default of patrilineal recognition: children are still usually expected to take the father’s surname, and family continuity is imagined through the male line.[5][6][14]
- intergenerational control over property: housing down payments, renovation, bride price, dowry, ancestral homes, land, and homestead-related claims are redistributed through marriage and parental intervention.[8][9][10][11]
- the stickiness of care work: even after women enter professional life, they are still more likely to be treated as the natural bearers of domestic work, childcare, visits, and emotional mediation.[3][4]
- changes in belonging: marriage migration can change a woman’s relation to her natal family, natal village, and natal property lines, especially in rural settings.[8][9]

So the ancestral hall is only an entry point, not the whole object. If the issue is named only as an ancestral hall problem, many readers will incorrectly assume that it does not concern them as long as they do not live in a strongly lineage-based environment.

## 2. Do these problems still objectively exist today?

The short answer is yes. But they do not exist in exactly the same form everywhere.

### 1. In cities, the problem becomes more implicit, not absent

According to the National Bureau of Statistics, China’s urbanization rate of the resident population reached `67.00%` in 2024.[1] That means it is no longer accurate to imagine China mainly as a landscape of visibly lineage-based villages. But it is equally wrong to conclude that patrilineal kinship order has automatically disappeared with urbanization.

In cities, the issue is usually carried not by an ancestral hall but by scenes such as:

- which family pays the housing down payment, whose name goes on the deed, and how the mortgage is repaid
- how a child’s surname is decided and whether grandparents hold strong expectations about it
- who takes leave after childbirth, who slows down work, and who becomes responsible for pickup and daily care
- whose family gets holiday priority, and who takes on more intense care and coordination when parents become ill

These often look like communication problems. In reality, they are distribution problems. The form changes, while the underlying logic remains. Visible lineage authority steps back; informal bargaining power, funding power, and default responsibility move forward.

### 2. In county seats, the problem looks more like a marriage market tied to intergenerational resources

County seats are one of the most concentrated spaces of conflict today. They connect urbanization and educational mobility on one side and strong parental intervention plus dense acquaintance networks on the other. Here the ancestral hall may not be visually central, but housing, bride price, wedding display, respectability, and staffing status often organize marriage more intensely than in major cities.

Bride price repeatedly becomes a public issue not because the state suddenly cares about private romance, but because it is tied to marriage-market competition, family risk transfer, and grassroots stability. Central governance documents and local data from Guyuan both show that high bride price is treated as an actual governance issue rather than as an accidental custom.[10][11]

What makes the county-seat setting distinctive is that many seemingly personal choices are made in an environment where both sets of parents can see everything, where acquaintances can judge everything, and where intergenerational resources intervene strongly. That is why surnames, bride price, housing, and post-marital residence are so easily bound together.

### 3. In rural areas, the problem can still appear in the most explicit form

Rural China is not a vanished past. The 2024 Migrant Workers Monitoring Report shows that the total number of migrant workers remained close to `300 million` in 2024.[2] That means a huge number of families are still split between the place of work and the place of origin: people work in cities while their hometowns still hold ancestral homes, land, homestead claims, elder-care obligations, and village membership ties.

Under those conditions, ancestral halls, genealogies, village rules, membership status, the treatment of married-out daughters, and land-related rights do not simply lose force when younger people move to cities. They can reappear sharply around demolition compensation, collective dividends, contracted land, homestead eligibility, elder care, or the decision to return and build.[8][9]

So the claim that “everything is urbanized now, ancestral halls no longer matter” is at best true for part of urban middle-class life. It is not a sufficient description of the broader Chinese social structure.

### 4. Unequal domestic and care labor is the most stable and most underestimated fact

The third national time-use survey shows that women spend much more time on domestic labor than men: `3 hours 29 minutes` for women compared with `1 hour 52 minutes` for men. Women also show higher participation and greater time investment in child care.[3]

This matters because it keeps us from misreading the exhaustion of office-working women as an individual failure. Many women already operate by the standards of a modern professional during the workday, then are assumed to take on a second shift after work: cooking, organizing, caring for elders, arranging children’s schedules, and maintaining relations between both families. The issue is not only that somebody said a few sexist lines. The issue is the concrete distribution of time and responsibility.

### 5. Entering the professional system does not automatically mean exiting kinship order

The statistical monitoring report on the Women’s Development Program shows continued gains in women’s education, employment, professional roles, and management participation.[4] But this does not mean women have entered a world detached from family reproduction as complete subjects. A more accurate description is that they have entered professional institutions while still being required to carry large parts of the traditional reproductive order.

That is why many educated office women in provincial capitals live with an intense contradiction: at work they are expected to be efficient, disciplined, professional, and reportable; at home they are expected to be softer, more understanding, better at care, and more willing to do a little more.

### 6. Rural women’s land rights and membership issues prove that marriage migration is never purely private

The Rural Collective Economic Organizations Law and related release materials, together with prosecutorial materials on rural women’s land rights, show that marital status is indeed tied to membership status, contracted land, homestead eligibility, and collective income distribution.[8][9]

This line matters because it shows that marriage is never only a matter between two individuals. It can also change a woman’s relation to natal resources, identity, belonging, and future security. In cities this line is often more deeply covered over, but it does not disappear.

## 3. What is the political economy behind these problems?

If we say only patriarchal leftovers, the explanation remains too thin. These problems persist not only because ideas remain conservative, but because a broader political-economic structure continues to support them.

### 1. High housing costs turn marriage back into a risk-sharing mechanism

In many cities and county seats, marriage remains a major mechanism for sharing housing, childcare, and elder-care risk. As long as housing down payments, renovation, wedding expenses, and post-birth support still depend heavily on both sets of parents, parents are not just emotional participants. They become co-authors of the distribution rules. That is why surnames, wedding scale, holiday arrangements, child-rearing choices, and elder-care obligations become sites of conflict: they are all conditions attached to resource entry.

### 2. Large-scale labor mobility has not ended. It produces a double structure of obligation

The 2024 migrant-worker report shows that large-scale population movement is still a basic fact.[2] That means the workplace, the marital household, and the natal family location often do not overlap. A woman working in a provincial capital may therefore face three simultaneous demands:

- the workplace expects her to function like a fully available professional
- the small household expects her to absorb stable domestic and care work
- the natal family and the husband’s family both expect continued participation in visits, rituals, elder care, and return-home obligations

This is not just bad time management. It is reproductive labor being compressed across different geographies into the same person.

### 3. Domestic and emotional labor are naturalized, which makes them harder to see

One reason unpaid labor remains so persistent is that it is often named as love, responsibility, and maturity rather than as work and expenditure.[3][4] Once a task is wrapped in phrases such as “you are more careful,” “you are better at care,” or “you communicate better,” it becomes harder to treat as labor that should be redistributed. That is exactly where many professional women get trapped: they mistake high-intensity emotional labor for a personality obligation.

### 4. Membership rights and return-home lines allow the natal structure to flow back in

As long as the family remains tied to land, ancestral housing, village membership, elder care, ritual return, or future building decisions, the thought that “I work in a provincial capital now, so this has little to do with me” will eventually break down.[8][9] The place of origin is not a dead past. It is a structural background that can return suddenly at key moments.

### 5. State governance itself acknowledges the reality of these problems

Whether we look at high bride-price governance, rule of law plus ancestral hall plus culture, or ancestral hall plus local worthies governance cases, the point is the same: the state has not treated these issues as mere private morality. It keeps using governance techniques to manage intermediary organizations, family conflict, and local order.[10][11][12][13] That itself is evidence that the problem is stable and real enough to require governance.

## 4. How does the dominant ideology work?

The most common ideology now is usually not an open declaration that women must obey. It works in subtler forms.

### 1. The discourse of family common sense

Typical lines are:

- it is only natural that the child takes the father’s surname
- the husband’s family paid more, so it is normal for them to ask more
- women are better suited to childcare and parental care
- holidays are not the time to be so calculating

The effect of this discourse is to turn distributive questions into common-sense questions that do not seem worth disputing.

### 2. The discourse of automatic progress through modernization

The core narrative of “Ancestral Halls and Surname Rights” comes close to this. It recognizes the patriarchal background of ancestral halls, genealogies, and surname conventions, but it places major hope in technology, civilization, and gradual progress.[14]

The problem is not that this is wholly false. The problem is that it misses something crucial: modernization can reduce explicit lineage spaces while allowing unequal distribution to continue in more civilized, more respectable, and harder-to-refute forms. Fewer ancestral halls do not automatically mean fairer arrangements around deeds, surnames, domestic work, return-home obligations, or elder care.

### 3. The discourse of legal formal equality as cover

Another common argument is that the law does not forbid a woman from keeping her own surname, and the law does not require the child to take the father’s surname, so China is already quite equal. This is only partly sufficient. Legal boundaries do not automatically become everyday bargaining capacity.[5][6] Many women know that the law does not fully support the family default, but they still cannot bear the cost of sustained negotiation alone.

## 5. How can Lacan’s four discourses help read these conflicts?

I use the four discourses here only as a working diagnostic.

- `Master discourse`: grandparents’ expectations, tradition, respectability, and family rules occupy the commanding position. It may not always sound loud, but it compresses the issue into “this is simply how it should be.”
- `University discourse`: legal language, policy language, statistical improvement, family counseling, and grassroots governance language provide explanation. They can provide order without necessarily changing distribution.
- `Hysteric discourse`: fatigue, complaint, resentment, questioning, and self-blame express the conflict. Many educated women remain here for a long time because they can see the injustice but cannot find a sufficiently low-cost exit.
- `Analyst discourse`: the issue is forced back onto concrete distribution. Who paid, who repays, who picks up the child, whose family gets holiday priority, who keeps natal ties, and where the record goes if conflict emerges.

What actually changes structure is not endless complaint. It is moving the issue as quickly as possible into that last position.

## 6. Why educated office women in provincial capitals especially need to relocate themselves

Their position is distinctive because they often combine three traits:

- relatively strong information skills and institutional literacy
- stable but highly draining office work
- a strong demand placed on themselves to manage both work and relationships well

Together these create a subtle trap. The more they understand the problem, the easier it becomes to keep consuming themselves through lines like “I should handle this more maturely” or “I should communicate better,” instead of demanding a redistribution of responsibility.

That is why many women who appear far freer than the previous generation are in fact carrying two systems at once: the metricized role of the modern professional by day, and the lubricating role of the traditional family by night. The problem is not that they never resist. The problem is that resistance is too costly, too fragmented, and too hard to record.

## 7. What does a more realistic F4 route look like?

By `F4` I do not mean a grand slogan. I mean a route better suited to ordinary working life: moving from solitary pressure-bearing to small-scale collaboration and institutional interfaces; moving from vague discomfort to explicit boundaries; moving from emotional exhaustion alone to evidence, templates, and relay.

This route can begin with five modules.

### 1. The naming and surname module

Do not treat names and child surnames as trivial symbols. They are often the first site where narrative authority inside the family becomes visible.[5][6] Practical moves include:

- discuss the issue before marriage or pregnancy rather than after pregnancy
- write the question into several concrete options instead of starting with abstract positions
- connect surname decisions to childcare responsibility, care investment, and the roles of grandparents

### 2. The property-boundary module

Housing down payments, mortgages, renovations, bride price, dowry, and gift transfers should be recorded as clearly as possible. This is not because distrust is the goal. It is because without records, every inequality can be retranslated as “you are being too calculating.” The way to reduce conflict is not to pretend not to care, but to make money sources, uses, and future obligations explicit.

### 3. The domestic-labor and childcare module

The statistics already tell us that inequality is not exceptional.[3] That is why domestic labor and childcare should be turned into responsibility sheets early rather than left to whoever happens to notice the task. For office workers in particular, vague arrangements are what most easily turn women’s after-work hours into a second shift.

### 4. The return-home and both-parents module

Who goes to whose family on holidays, who accompanies which parent to the hospital, and how future elder care will be distributed should not be left to whoever is more understanding. The better approach is to put frequency, duration, costs, and leave-taking burdens openly on the table.

### 5. The natal-membership and property-trace module

As long as one’s hometown still involves land, ancestral housing, homestead claims, collective distribution, or elder care, one should not assume that these lines are irrelevant.[8][9] Many conflicts remain silent in ordinary times and erupt only around demolition, dividends, inheritance, divorce, or elder-care crises. The earlier they are clarified, the less passive the position becomes.

## 8. Why the F4 route is not too practical to be radical

Some readers may think these moves are too detailed, too practical, or too much like everyday management to count as real resistance. That misses an important point. The order of kinship, property, and recognition is usually not maintained mainly by open violence. It is maintained by dispersed, flexible arrangements that can be named as love or respectability. If the structure works through those small sites, practice also has to work there.

That is exactly why `F4` matters:

- it does not require a person to complete some abstract consciousness exam first
- it recognizes the real time and energy limits of office work
- it turns personal pain into problems that can be negotiated, recorded, and relayed
- it allows `2-5` people in the same city and life stage to reality-check one another and reduce self-doubt

That is not a conservative route. It is closer to the kind of change that can actually continue.

## 9. Closing

“Ancestral Halls and Surname Rights” matters not because it already gives a mature answer, but because it touches a real entrance to the structure.[14] But if we are satisfied with the story that tradition will slowly retreat through civilization and progress, we miss the most important reality of the present: patrilineal kinship order has not simply exited. It has kept changing carriers, moving from ancestral halls, genealogies, and surname conventions into property deeds, domestic labor, childcare, return-home obligations, membership status, and intergenerational support.

For educated office women in provincial capitals, the decisive step is not to prove that they are more modern than the previous generation. It is to admit first that they are not outside the structure. Only then does it become clearer why the problem appears exactly when one is busiest, most tired, and least willing to fight; and only then can abstract grievance begin to turn into concrete boundary work.

If the general diagnosis is `F2-O2-E1-T1`, then the more realistic practical target is to push life gradually toward `F4-O3-E3-T3`: from a semi-institutional kinship field toward small-scale collaboration and institutional relay; from role absorption toward clearer subject boundaries; from common sense and emotion toward evidence and procedure; and from merely keeping the relationship running toward making burdens and entitlements inside it more sustainable.

The ancestral hall may not stand in front of you. But the order it left behind is very likely sitting just outside the office, beside the dinner table, on the housing deed, in the calendar, and inside the return-home plan. The real work is not to wait for that order to leave on its own. It is to learn how to rewrite it, inch by inch, in all of those places.
